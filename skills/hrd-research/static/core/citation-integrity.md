# Citation Integrity

Use this file for every HRD research writing task. Citation handling is evidence integrity, not decoration.

## Non-Negotiable Rules

- Preserve all user-provided in-text citations unless the user explicitly asks to remove them.
- Keep each citation adjacent to the specific claim it supports.
- Do not invent citations, publication years, page numbers, DOIs, authors, or reference details.
- Do not add a citation to a claim unless the user supplied that citation or explicitly asked for a placeholder.
- Do not strengthen a cited claim beyond what the sentence originally supported.

## When Revising

If you move a claim, move its citation with it.

If you split one cited sentence into multiple sentences, place the citation where the supported claim appears. Duplicate the citation only when the same source clearly supports both revised claims.

If you merge multiple cited sentences, keep citations attached to the corresponding parts of the merged sentence. Do not collapse several different citations into one broad end citation unless each source supports the whole merged claim.

If you generalize a claim, add causal language, add comparison language, or broaden the population/context, flag the change as needing author verification.

## High-Risk Claim Types

Require adjacent citation support or user-provided evidence for:

- definitions of HRD constructs
- theoretical mechanisms
- empirical findings from prior research
- claims about what prior literature has shown
- claims about gaps, limitations, or consensus in the field
- cross-cultural or international generalizations
- causal, comparative, or evaluative claims
- practice recommendations based on evidence

## Output Expectations

When citation risk exists, include a short "Citation checks" or "Items to verify" note.

Use labels such as:

- `[citation needed]`
- `[verify citation support]`
- `[source does not clearly support strengthened claim]`
- `[author: confirm whether this citation supports the full sentence]`

Do not hide citation uncertainty by making prose sound smoother.
